\section{Discussion and Outlook}
\label{sec:discussion}
We have already seen in section~\ref{Temperature_dependency} that the Metropolis algorithm boasts higher variance in the measured observables.
It turns out that the amount of data recording steps $N_{Steps}$ are too low to obtain accurate results and reduce the variance.
Longer measurements $N_{Steps}$ and more separate repetitions $N_{Rep}$ can be made to reduce noise and improve the accuracy of these results.
\par
Furthermore, it has also been shown that the autocorrelation times $\tau_{\text{exp}}$ increase significantly around and below the critical temperature.
To improve the accuracy of measurements, instead of continuously recording the data after the burn-in phase, one should wait $\tau_{\text{exp}}$ time steps between measurements, and only record for spin configurations that are statistically independend.
However this would also increase the computational time, especially for the Metropolis algorithm, where $\tau_{\text{exp}}$ is significantly larger around $T_c$.
The Wolff algorithm would benefit less from this improvement, as its correlation time is already at order $\tau_{\text{exp}} \sim 10^0$.
\par
Performing the data collapse by visual judgement proved to be more difficult due to the scaling of larger lattice sizes resulting in fewer usable points around the peak.
This could be remdied by deploying a finer temperature grid.
In order to limit the increased computational time that would result from this, instead of using equidistant points, distributing them more around the critical temperature and less in the regions around, one could effectively improve the quality of the curves without the increased computational cost.
\par
For further improvements a combination of CUDA and Pytorch could be used to further speed up the simulations, especially for larger lattice sizes.
This would allow for more measurements to be taken in a given time frame, improving overall accuracy.
Overall, the Wolff algorithm is more efficient and accurate for studying the XY model and its phase transition compared to the Metropolis algorithm, especially near the critical temperature where critical slowing down is most pronounced.
